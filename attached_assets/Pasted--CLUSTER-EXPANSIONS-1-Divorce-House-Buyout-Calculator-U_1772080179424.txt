Divorce House Buyout Calculator UK – Property Retention Scenario Modelling

The family home is often the most significant financial asset in divorce. Whether one party retains the property or it is sold can materially affect liquidity, debt exposure, and long-term sustainability.

In England & Wales, there is no automatic right to retain property. Outcomes are determined within a discretionary legal framework. High-level guidance is available via GOV.UK.

Structured financial modelling can illustrate the numerical consequences of retaining or selling the home under defined assumptions.

It does not determine legal entitlement.

⸻

Step 1: Calculating Net Equity

Property modelling begins with net equity:

Market value
– Outstanding mortgage
– Estimated sale costs
= Net equity

Example:
	•	Property value: £600,000
	•	Mortgage: £180,000
	•	Sale costs (illustrative): £12,000
Net equity ≈ £408,000

This forms part of the broader marital asset pool.

⸻

Step 2: Buyout Mechanics

If one party retains the home, they may need to:
	•	Refinance the mortgage in sole name
	•	Release equity to compensate the other party
	•	Offset against other assets (e.g., pension)

Example:

If total net pool = £800,000
Equal division = £400,000 each

If one party keeps £408,000 equity home, they may owe balancing capital elsewhere.

Modelling allows this to be visualised numerically.

⸻

Step 3: Mortgage Affordability Assessment

Affordability modelling may consider:
	•	Gross annual income
	•	General income multiple benchmarks (often ~4–4.5x gross income, illustrative only)
	•	Existing debt
	•	Interest rate sensitivity

Example:

Income: £70,000
4.5x multiple ≈ £315,000 (illustrative only)

If required borrowing exceeds this range, retention may require additional capital or alternative structuring.

These benchmarks are not lending decisions.

⸻

Step 4: Liquidity & Concentration Risk

Retaining the property may create:
	•	High asset concentration
	•	Reduced accessible cash
	•	Increased vulnerability to interest rate shifts

Example:

£408,000 in property
£20,000 in savings

Liquidity imbalance becomes apparent.

Structured modelling can test alternative scenarios:
	•	Property sale
	•	Partial equity release
	•	Different percentage splits

⸻

Step 5: Sustainability Modelling

Long-term sustainability may involve testing:
	•	2% interest increase
	•	Income reduction
	•	Increased childcare costs
	•	Maintenance obligations

This transforms property retention analysis from static division to forward-looking viability.

⸻

Sale Scenario Comparison

Selling the property may:
	•	Release capital
	•	Reduce debt exposure
	•	Increase liquidity flexibility

However, it may require rehousing and incur transaction costs.

Side-by-side comparison provides clarity.

Divorce Pension Split Calculator UK – Retirement Asset Modelling

Pensions are often one of the largest components of marital wealth. Their treatment can significantly influence both immediate capital distribution and long-term retirement outcomes.

High-level information on pension sharing orders is available via GOV.UK.

Structured modelling can illustrate numerical differences between pension sharing and offsetting assumptions.

⸻

Pension Sharing

Pension sharing typically involves:
	•	Assigning a percentage of one pension fund
	•	Creating an independent pension pot for the other party

Example:

Pension CETV: £300,000
50% share → £150,000 transfer

Modelling can illustrate resulting asset totals and long-term capital structure.

⸻

Pension Offsetting

Offsetting may involve:
	•	One party retaining pension assets
	•	The other retaining more property or cash

Example:

Party A retains £300,000 pension
Party B receives additional £300,000 property equity

Short-term liquidity differs materially from long-term retirement value.

⸻

Liquidity vs Retirement Security

Pensions are:
	•	Long-term
	•	Illiquid
	•	Subject to retirement age access rules

Offsetting may increase immediate capital while reducing long-term retirement provision.

Modelling allows numerical comparison without recommending an approach.

⸻

Tax Considerations

Pension tax treatment is complex and scheme-dependent.

Simplified modelling does not fully account for:
	•	Lifetime allowance history
	•	Scheme-specific rules
	•	Early access penalties
	•	Detailed tax relief calculations

Tax modelling remains illustrative only.

⸻

Sustainability Impact

Pension-heavy allocations may require:
	•	Alternative capital planning
	•	Interim liquidity assessment
	•	Retirement timeline modelling

Structured comparison helps clarify these trade-offs.

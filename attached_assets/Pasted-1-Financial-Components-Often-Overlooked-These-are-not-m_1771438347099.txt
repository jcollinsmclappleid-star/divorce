1️⃣ Financial Components Often Overlooked

These are not mandatory — but you should consciously decide.

⸻

A) Spousal Maintenance (Periodical Payments)

Currently your model is capital-focused.

In many UK divorces, ongoing maintenance materially affects:
	•	Monthly surplus
	•	Runway
	•	Stability classification

If not included, you must explicitly state:

“Ongoing spousal maintenance not modelled unless entered manually.”

Otherwise users assume it’s captured.

⸻

B) Child Maintenance (CMS)

You mention CMS excluded.

This is not minor.

For many cases, it is one of the largest monthly cashflow shifts.

If excluded, you should:
	•	Either allow manual entry
	•	Or clearly state it is not included in modelling

Failure to do so risks misleading sustainability outputs.

⸻

C) Transition Costs

When selling:
	•	Moving costs
	•	Rental deposit
	•	Legal fees
	•	Stamp duty on new purchase

These can materially change runway in lower-liquidity cases.

At minimum:
Add an “Additional one-off costs” input field.

⸻

D) Pension Type Clarification

Are pension inputs:
	•	CETV (Cash Equivalent Transfer Value)?
	•	Defined Contribution only?
	•	Defined Benefit?

If not clarified, some users may treat DB pensions like cash assets.

You should include:

“Pension values reflect transfer values and not income stream equivalence.”

⸻

E) Capital Gains Tax on Investments

If you assume full liquidation with no tax:

That is unrealistic in some scenarios.

Either:
	•	Add optional tax haircut %
OR
	•	Clearly state CGT not modelled.

⸻

2️⃣ Structural Modelling Considerations

⸻

A) Double Counting Risk

You must ensure:
	•	Pensions excluded from liquid capital
	•	No debt duplicated across scenarios
	•	Selling costs applied once

This seems handled — but audit annually.

⸻

B) Stability Score Weighting

Currently deduction-based.

Ask yourself:

Is -40 funding shortfall proportionate relative to -10 asset concentration?

Your weights implicitly express value judgements.

You must be comfortable defending them.

⸻

C) Inflation vs Income Assumption

Clarify:

Are you inflating:
	•	Expenses only?
	•	Income only?
	•	Both?
	•	Neither?

Users will interpret differently.

State it clearly.

⸻

3️⃣ Legal / Regulatory Framing Gaps

Critical for UK context.

⸻

A) “Fairness” Language

Ensure nowhere you imply:
	•	This is a fair settlement
	•	This is likely outcome
	•	This reflects court practice

You are modelling sustainability, not fairness.

⸻

B) Disclaimer Placement

Too many disclaimers reduce trust.
Too few increase liability.

You need:
	•	Top banner
	•	Footer in report
	•	Assumptions section

Not more.

⸻

C) Data Retention & Privacy

Have you considered:
	•	Where user financial data is stored?
	•	Encryption?
	•	Retention period?
	•	GDPR compliance?
	•	Deletion mechanism?

This is often overlooked.

In divorce context, data sensitivity is extreme.

⸻

4️⃣ Product Positioning Blind Spots

⸻

A) Are You Modelling “Decision” or “Outcome”?

Right now, you model:

Sustainability.

Not:
	•	Likely court award
	•	Needs-based settlement
	•	Sharing principle outcomes

That’s good.

But your messaging must match that.

⸻

B) Emotional Weight

This tool is used during high stress.

You’ve added reflective prompts — good.

But ensure tone is:

Structured, not cold.

Analytical, not robotic.

⸻

5️⃣ The Biggest Strategic Blind Spot

The biggest risk you may not have considered:

User over-reliance.

If someone makes a major financial decision based solely on your tool, you must be able to demonstrate:
	•	It is modelling-based
	•	It is assumption-driven
	•	It is not predictive

That is where your liability lives — not in calculation bugs.

⸻

What You Do NOT Need

You do not need:
	•	Court outcome prediction
	•	Section 25 factor modelling
	•	Needs algorithm
	•	Solicitor recommendation engine
	•	Tax optimisation engine

That would increase risk dramatically.

⸻

As a divorce separation financial modelling tool, you are:

Technically strong
Structurally coherent
Commercially viable
Not over-engineered

The remaining gaps are:
	•	Maintenance handling clarity
	•	Transition cost realism
	•	Explicit assumption transparency
	•	Data protection strategy

Not core engine flaws.

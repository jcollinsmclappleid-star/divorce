I have a browser-based divorce financial modelling tool for England and Wales. It already produces a structured financial report with scenario modelling, income/tax outputs, settlement scenarios, sustainability indicators, mortgage benchmark checks, assumptions and methodology.

I want you to enhance the product by adding a safe AI-powered interpretation layer called:

Smart Report Notes

Important product boundary:
This tool must NOT provide legal advice, financial advice, tax advice, mortgage advice, settlement recommendations, court outcome predictions, or suitability recommendations.

The AI layer must only:
- explain the model outputs in plain English
- highlight pressure points shown by the figures
- identify missing information or assumptions that could affect the model
- generate neutral questions to ask a solicitor, mediator, mortgage broker, pension expert or tax adviser
- help the user understand what their own numbers show
- avoid telling the user what to do

The deterministic calculation engine must remain the source of truth. Do not use AI to calculate tax, NI, mortgage payments, child maintenance, asset division, pension splits, surplus/deficit, sustainability scores, or projections. AI should only digest already-calculated outputs.

Core objective:
Upgrade the existing dashboard/report experience so it feels like a premium guided financial interpretation report, while keeping the product safe, neutral and non-advisory.

Please inspect the current codebase and implement the following enhancements.

====================================================
2. SMART REPORT NOTES OUTPUT BLOCKS
====================================================

The AI output should be structured into these blocks:

A. Plain-English Overview

Purpose:
Explain the overall financial picture in simple, neutral language.

Example tone:
“This model shows that most of the household wealth is tied up in the family home. The main pressure point is not only the total net worth, but whether either party can retain the home while maintaining enough monthly cashflow and liquid capital.”

Rules:
- Do not recommend a scenario
- Do not say what is fair
- Do not say what a court would do
- Do not say either party should accept/reject anything

B. What Stands Out

Generate 4–6 bullet points based on the calculated model.

Examples:
- The family home is the largest asset in the model.
- One party has materially higher income than the other.
- A keep-home scenario creates higher mortgage pressure.
- A party may have limited liquid capital after an equity buyout.
- Pension figures are included, but pension type and actuarial treatment are not assessed.
- Some costs, such as moving costs or legal fees, may not be included.

C. Scenario Interpretation

For each available scenario, create:
- one short “scenario in one sentence”
- one short explanatory paragraph
- specific references to calculated figures where available

Scenarios may include:
- Sell & Split
- Party A Keeps Home
- Party B Keeps Home
- Deferred Sale

Rules:
- Use “from a cashflow perspective only” where comparing affordability
- Use “based on the figures entered”
- Use “may be a point to discuss”
- Do not identify any scenario as “best”, “recommended”, “fairest”, or “most suitable”

D. Pressure Points

Generate 3–6 pressure points from the model.

Pressure point types may include:
- mortgage affordability pressure
- low liquid capital after buyout
- high asset concentration in property
- income disparity
- pension imbalance
- missing children/maintenance assumptions
- missing transaction costs
- sensitivity to interest rate increases
- reliance on one party’s income

Each pressure point must be written neutrally.

Example:
“Mortgage pressure: In the Party B Keeps Home scenario, the model shows that the mortgage payment takes a high share of Party B’s net monthly income. This may be a useful point to discuss with a mortgage broker or solicitor.”

E. Questions to Ask Professionals

Generate separate question lists for:

1. Solicitor or mediator
2. Mortgage broker
3. Pension expert / financial adviser
4. Tax adviser, if tax complexity is flagged

Questions must be framed as questions, not recommendations.

Examples:
- “How would pension sharing or pension offsetting normally be considered where CETV values differ?”
- “If one party keeps the family home, what protections may be needed around mortgage responsibility and equity transfer?”
- “Could either party refinance the mortgage in their sole name based on income and debt levels?”
- “Would stamp duty, capital gains tax or investment disposal costs need to be considered?”

F. Missing Information & Model Confidence

Create a model confidence label:
- High
- Medium
- Low

This should be based on completeness of inputs, not certainty of outcome.

Use rule-based logic where possible. For example:

High:
- core income, property value, mortgage balance, main assets, liabilities and pensions provided
- child/spousal maintenance either not applicable or entered
- major housing costs included

Medium:
- core figures provided but some relevant assumptions missing
- examples: missing pension CETV for one party, missing childcare costs, missing interest rate, missing transaction costs

Low:
- significant missing information
- examples: missing income for one party, missing mortgage balance, missing major assets, missing pension information where likely relevant

The AI can write the explanation, but the confidence label should be determined by rule-based logic if possible.

Example:
“Model confidence: Medium. The model includes income, property and mortgage figures, but confidence is reduced because Party B pension CETV, childcare costs and transaction costs are missing.”

G. Suggested Preparation Steps

Generate a short checklist of 5–8 preparation steps.

Use wording:
“Suggested preparation steps”
not:
“Recommended actions”

Examples:
- Confirm current mortgage balance and any early repayment charge.
- Obtain current pension CETV figures.
- Add estimated rent or onward purchase costs for each party.
- Add legal, moving and transfer costs.
- Test the model at higher mortgage rates.
- Take the scenario comparison to a solicitor, mediator or mortgage broker.

3. SAFETY AND WORDING RULES
====================================================

Hard-block the following phrases or concepts from AI output:

- “you should”
- “you must”
- “we recommend”
- “recommended settlement”
- “best option”
- “best scenario”
- “fair settlement”
- “fairest”
- “entitled to”
- “court would”
- “court will”
- “likely court outcome”
- “accept this”
- “reject this”
- “legal advice”
- “financial advice”
- “tax advice”
- “mortgage advice”
- “suitable for you”
- “right choice”

If generated output contains these phrases, either:
- regenerate once with stricter instructions, or
- sanitise the wording into neutral language.

Preferred safe phrases:
- “based on the figures entered”
- “from a cashflow perspective only”
- “may be a point to discuss”
- “could be relevant to review”
- “this model shows”
- “this scenario illustrates”
- “professional advice may be needed before making decisions”
- “this does not assess legal fairness or suitability”

Add a visible note to Smart Report Notes:

“These notes explain the model outputs in plain English. They are not legal, financial, tax or mortgage advice, and they do not recommend what either party should do.”
